# Homepage Image Direction

## 1. Overall Visual Style

The homepage should feel warm, clear, organized, and trustworthy for families of K-8 students. Images should show students actively learning with visible adult guidance. The page should look like a serious enrichment academy, not a college site, software dashboard ad, or generic technology startup.

Use images that show:

- K-8 students in age-appropriate learning settings
- Small-group instruction
- Hands-on work
- Chess, coding, robotics, math, or AI literacy activities
- Parent-visible progress when dashboard screenshots are shown
- Calm, natural light where possible
- Diverse students and families
- Clear learning materials in the frame

## 2. Hero Image Direction

The hero can use the existing rotating image carousel, but the images should support the plain-language promise: K-8 enrichment in chess, coding, robotics, math, and AI literacy.

Recommended hero carousel images:

1. A student working with an instructor on a STEM activity
2. Elementary students learning coding in a small group
3. Students studying a chessboard during a strategy lesson
4. Students building or testing a small robotics project
5. Students working through a math or maker challenge together

Avoid images where the main action is cropped out by the 3D carousel shape.

## 3. Carousel Image Direction

Each carousel image should clearly represent a program area.

- Chess: student and board visible, thoughtful posture, instructor or peer nearby
- Coding: student project visible, screen present but not dominating the image
- Robotics: students handling components, testing a small build, or making a revision
- Math: visible puzzle, whiteboard, pattern activity, or collaborative reasoning task
- AI Literacy: student and teacher discussing technology in an age-appropriate setting

Keep faces, hands, and materials visible. Avoid images that only show laptops, devices, or generic classroom scenes.

## 4. Program Section Image Direction

Each program card image should answer: "What will my child do here?"

Use one strong image per program area:

- Chess: student planning a move with the board visible
- Coding: student building a project with guidance
- Robotics: students assembling or testing a small build
- Math: students solving a reasoning challenge together
- AI Literacy: teacher-guided discussion or activity about technology choices

Avoid images that look staged, overly polished, or unrelated to the program.

## 5. Chess Image Direction

Use a photo of a student and instructor working through a chess position. The chessboard should be easy to see.

Good image options:

- Child considering a move
- Two students discussing a position
- Instructor pointing to a board
- Small chess group around a table

Avoid:

- Adult-only chess photos
- Dark dramatic chess imagery
- Tournament scenes that feel intimidating
- Children who look bored, confused, or isolated

## 6. Coding Image Direction

Use a photo of students creating a project on a laptop with an instructor or peer nearby. The image should communicate guided creation, not isolated screen time.

Good image options:

- Student showing a simple game or animation
- Pair programming with students in the K-8 range
- Instructor helping a student debug
- Child explaining a project

Avoid:

- Generic laptop close-ups
- Adult developer desk imagery
- Complex adult code as the main visual
- Students who look older than the intended K-8 audience

## 7. Robotics Image Direction

Use a photo of students building, testing, or revising a small robotics project.

Good image options:

- Students assembling a robot
- Teacher guiding a robotics challenge
- Students testing a small build
- Components, wheels, sensors, blocks, or wires visible

Avoid:

- Industrial robots
- Sci-fi humanoid robots
- Scary robot or AI imagery
- Cluttered tables where the project cannot be identified

## 8. Math Image Direction

Use visuals that show reasoning, patterns, and problem-solving. Math should feel active and guided, not worksheet-heavy.

Good image options:

- Students solving a puzzle together
- Teacher guiding whiteboard reasoning
- Child using manipulatives, grids, or pattern cards
- Small-group math challenge

Avoid:

- Worksheets as the main visual
- Calculator stock photos
- Advanced equations that imply high school or college
- A child looking frustrated alone

## 9. AI Literacy Image Direction

AI literacy should look guided, responsible, and age-appropriate.

Good image options:

- Teacher-led discussion about technology
- Students comparing examples or prompts
- Child using a tablet or laptop with adult guidance
- Visual thinking activity about when tools are helpful

Avoid:

- Robot faces
- Glowing brains
- Dystopian technology imagery
- Hacker imagery
- Abstract AI graphics as the main image

## 10. Parent Dashboard Image Direction

Use a real parent dashboard screenshot once private data is removed. Until then, use a clearly approved product mockup.

The dashboard visual should show:

- Child profile or student list
- Enrolled programs
- Upcoming class
- Progress status
- Lessons, quizzes, assignments, or certificates
- Message or notification area

Avoid dense analytics visuals that look like a corporate software product.

## 11. Student Experience Image Direction

Show students participating, explaining, building, or presenting work.

Good image options:

- Student explaining a project
- Small group collaboration
- Teacher coaching a child
- Child sharing a result or build
- Online class moment that shows actual interaction

Avoid passive screenshots of video-call grids unless the interaction is clear.

## 12. Safety / Trust Image Direction

Trust visuals should feel human and practical.

Good image options:

- Teacher guiding a small group
- Parent reviewing progress with a child
- Calm online learning setup at home
- Clear adult presence in a class environment

Avoid using padlock graphics as the main trust image. If trust badges are shown, every claim must be approved.

## 13. School Partnership Image Direction

Show school or community learning contexts with students and an instructor.

Good image options:

- After-school enrichment class
- Teacher leading robotics or chess club
- Students collaborating in a classroom or library
- School leader observing a program

Avoid university lecture halls, corporate training rooms, or empty classrooms.

## 14. Final CTA Image Direction

The final CTA can use a warm image of a student sharing work, a parent and child reviewing progress, or a small group completing a challenge.

The image should make the next step feel clear and low-pressure.

## 15. Alt Text Suggestions

Hero carousel:

- "K-8 student working with a teacher on a STEM activity"
- "Elementary students learning coding in a small group"
- "Students studying a chessboard during a strategy lesson"
- "Students building a robotics project with instructor guidance"
- "Students collaborating on a hands-on math challenge"

Program cards:

- "Child planning a chess move during a lesson"
- "Student building a coding project on a laptop"
- "Students testing a small robotics build"
- "Students solving a math reasoning challenge together"
- "Teacher guiding students through an age-appropriate AI literacy activity"

Dashboard:

- "Parent dashboard showing child progress and upcoming classes"

School partnership:

- "Students participating in an after-school enrichment program"

## 16. Images to Avoid

Avoid:

- Students who look older than K-8
- Generic laptop stock photos
- Corporate software dashboard visuals
- University-style lecture imagery
- Scary AI or robot imagery
- Cluttered classrooms where the activity is unclear
- Daycare-style images
- Dark STEM lab imagery
- Adults-only education visuals
- Abstract AI graphics as primary imagery
- Children looking isolated, stressed, or confused

Required before public launch:

- Final hero carousel images
- Program card images for each active program
- Parent dashboard screenshot or approved placeholder
- Student experience images
- Real or approved testimonial images only if testimonials are real
- School partnership image if school CTA remains on homepage
- Approved trust or compliance badges only if claims are verified

Can wait until after first launch:

- Detailed teacher dashboard screenshots
- Cohort-specific class photos
- Event or camp photography
- Expanded school partnership gallery
- Certificate close-up imagery
- Blog or editorial image library
